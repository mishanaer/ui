# Black holes

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity pulls so hard that
**nothing** — not even light — can escape. They form when a massive star runs
out of fuel and collapses, crushing its core into a point of seemingly
*infinite* density.

![The M87 black hole, imaged by the Event Horizon Telescope](https://eventhorizontelescope.org/m87.jpg)

## Anatomy

Every black hole is built from a few defining parts:

- **Singularity** — the central point where density runs to infinity
- **Event horizon** — the point of no return; cross it and you can never come back
- **Accretion disk** — superheated gas spiraling inward, blazing across the spectrum
- **Photon sphere** — where gravity bends light into a ring around the shadow

> A black hole has no hair: once formed, it is described by just three numbers —
> mass, charge, and spin.

### The event horizon

The radius of the horizon is called the *Schwarzschild radius*, and remarkably
it depends only on mass. You can write it with a famously short formula:

```
r_s = 2 * G * M / c**2
```

For the Sun that radius is just under 3 km — squeeze the entire Sun inside a
small town and it would wink out as a black hole. Double the mass and you double
the radius; the horizon grows in lockstep with whatever falls in.

## A question of scale

Black holes come in wildly different sizes, from collapsed stars to the giants
anchoring entire galaxies:

| Type | Mass | Radius |
| :--- | :--- | ---: |
| Stellar | 3–100 ☉ | ~30 km |
| Intermediate | 10³–10⁵ ☉ | ~10³ km |
| Supermassive | 10⁶–10¹⁰ ☉ | up to AU |

The giant at the center of our galaxy, `Sgr A*`, weighs about 4 million Suns.
The first one ever imaged, `M87*`, is a true monster at 6.5 billion.

## How we "see" the invisible

You cannot see a black hole directly, so astronomers infer it three ways:

1. Watch stars whip around an unseen mass and weigh what must be there
2. Catch gravitational waves from two black holes merging
3. Image the shadow it casts on the glowing gas around the horizon

The third method is the newest. In 2019 the [Event Horizon
Telescope](https://eventhorizontelescope.org) linked radio dishes across the
planet into one Earth-sized instrument and captured the first photo of a
shadow — the one cast by `M87*`.

> If you fell in, a distant observer would watch you *freeze* and redden at the
> horizon forever, while you yourself would cross it without noticing a thing.

---

Black holes sit at the strange seam where relativity and quantum mechanics
refuse to agree — which is exactly why physicists keep staring into them.
